## Session Overview
This session covers five practical interview preparation tips shared by an AWS Enterprise Solutions Architect who conducts interviews professionally and has been through numerous interview cycles himself.

## Q1
**Question:** What is the first tip for preparing for interviews, and how should you approach researching potential questions?

**Answer:** Research Glassdoor for the positions you are interviewing for. Even though you won't get an exact match, it gives you an idea of what kind of questions to expect. Create a list of questions gathered from Glassdoor, put them in an Excel spreadsheet, deduplicate them, and think about follow-up questions that could arise from the set. Also practice your delivery to be flawless for questions you know.

**Ideal Interview Answer:**
Before any interview, research Glassdoor extensively for the target position. Compile all questions into a spreadsheet, remove duplicates, and develop comprehensive answers including use cases, pros/cons, and follow-up scenarios. Then practice your delivery until it's flawless—either mentally or in front of a mirror. This preparation ensures you can confidently answer any question from your preparation list.

**Underlying Concept:**
Glassdoor provides real candidate experiences revealing the types of questions companies ask. The goal isn't memorizing exact questions but understanding patterns, depth, and areas of focus that interviewers target.

**Real-World Use Cases:**
- Preparing for a DevOps role and discovering recurring questions about CI/CD pipelines, containerization, and infrastructure as code
- Finding that AWS Solutions Architect interviews frequently cover high availability patterns, cost optimization, and security best practices
- Noticing database-focused positions emphasize trade-offs between SQL and NoSQL, scalability scenarios, and disaster recovery strategies

**Advantages:**
- Reduces interview anxiety by familiarizing you with question patterns
-Allows targeted preparation on high-frequency topics
- Reveals company-specific technical focus areas
- Helps identify knowledge gaps before the actual interview
- Provides context on expected depth and complexity levels

**Disadvantages:**
- Questions may not exactly match what you'll face
- Some Glassdoor entries may be outdated or inaccurate
- Requires significant time investment to compile and practice
- Over-preparation on specific questions may miss broader concepts

**Common Misconceptions:**
- Expecting exact questions to appear in interviews
- Assuming all Glassdoor questions are equally likely
- Not preparing for follow-up or related questions
- Ignoring the need to practice delivery, not just content

## Q2
**Question:** Why is giving yourself adequate preparation time important, and how long should you prepare?

**Answer:** Don't rush into interviews immediately after deciding you want a new job. Give yourself time to prepare all questions, brush up algorithms for coding interviews, study company leadership principles, and mentally prepare for intense interview processes. The speaker prepared for three months before applying to Amazon. It's not about how much you already know—it's about practicing questions and building mental endurance for potentially six rounds in one day.

**Ideal Interview Answer:**
Allocate sufficient preparation time before applying—typically 2-3 months depending on the role complexity. Use this time to systematically work through potential questions, practice coding challenges if applicable, study the company's leadership principles, and build mental stamina for multi-round interview days. The preparation period should focus on organized practice, not just passive knowledge accumulation.

**Underlying Concept:**
Interview performance depends more on practiced delivery and mental preparation than existing knowledge. Building endurance and refining communication requires dedicated practice time that cannot be rushed.

**Real-World Use Cases:**
- Dedicating 3 months before Amazon interviews to study leadership principles and practice behavioral responses
- Spending 6 weeks preparing for coding interviews by solving algorithm problems daily
- Using 2 months to research and prepare answers aligned with a target company's core values and technical focus

**Advantages:**
- Allows thorough coverage of all potential topics
- Builds confidence through repeated practice
- Enables identification and filling of knowledge gaps
- Develops mental stamina for demanding interview schedules
- Improves answer quality through iteration and refinement

**Disadvantages:**
- Delays job search timeline
- Requires sustained motivation over extended periods
- May become outdated if company processes change
- Can create overthinking or anxiety if preparation extends too long

**Common Misconceptions:**
- Assuming current job experience is sufficient preparation
- Believing you can "wing it" based on existing knowledge
- Underestimating the mental endurance required for full-day interviews
- Focusing only on technical content without practicing delivery

## Q3
**Question:** How should you research your interviewers, and what benefits does this provide?

**Answer:** When the recruiter provides interviewer names a couple days before, look them up on LinkedIn. Analyze their experience, skills, and focus areas to identify their expertise. If an interviewer has deep database experience across Oracle, Exadata, GoldenGate, and RAC, you should brush up on database knowledge and avoid faking expertise in their specialty area.

**Ideal Interview Answer:**
Research each interviewer on LinkedIn to understand their technical background, current role, and areas of expertise. This intelligence helps you anticipate question types and prepare accordingly. Never claim deep expertise in an interviewer's specialty area unless you genuinely possess it—they can easily detect inauthenticity.

**Underlying Concept:**
Interviewers tend to ask deeper questions in their areas of expertise. Understanding their background allows you to prepare for likely question domains and avoid overclaiming knowledge in their specialty areas.

**Real-World Use Cases:**
- Discovering an interviewer specializes in distributed systems and preparing accordingly
- Identifying that your panel includes a former database architect and reviewing advanced database concepts
- Noting an interviewer's background in security and preparing to discuss security best practices across your projects

**Advantages:**
- Provides insight into likely question areas
- Helps calibrate the depth of answers expected
- Prevents embarrassing situations where you fake knowledge
- Shows professionalism and preparation
- Enables more targeted and relevant answers

**Disadvantages:**
- May create bias or preconceptions about interviewers
- Information might be outdated or incomplete
- Could create anxiety if interviewers seem highly specialized
- Time investment required for thorough research

**Common Misconceptions:**
- Assuming interviewers only ask about their current role
- Not realizing the depth interviewers can probe in their expertise areas
- Believing you can successfully fake knowledge with experienced interviewers
- Ignoring that interviewer expertise extends beyond current job titles

## Q4
**Question:** Why is it important to remember that interviews are not tests, and how should you approach situations where you don't know answers?

**Answer:** Good interviewers assess how you think and approach problems, not whether you answer 100% correctly. The speaker has hired candidates who didn't answer all questions right but demonstrated strong problem-solving approaches. Never exaggerate or lie about your knowledge—being caught in a lie is worse than admitting gaps. When self-assessing expertise, be honest because interviewers will probe claimed areas of depth.

**Ideal Interview Answer:**
Approach interviews as conversations about problem-solving rather than tests with right answers. When you encounter unknown areas, acknowledge gaps honestly and explain your reasoning process for solving unfamiliar problems. Never inflate expertise levels—interviewers use self-assessments to choose question areas, not to evaluate your worthiness.

**Underlying Concept:**
Technical interviews evaluate problem-solving processes, logical thinking, and communication skills more than memorized knowledge. Admitting unknowns while demonstrating structured thinking often impresses interviewers more than perfect but inauthentic answers.

**Real-World Use Cases:**
- Encountering an unfamiliar technology and walking through a systematic approach to learn and implement it
- Admitting limited experience with a specific database while explaining how you'd approach capacity planning for an unknown system
- Being honest about not having production experience with Kubernetes while describing how you'd approach troubleshooting based on general containerization knowledge

**Advantages:**
- Builds authentic rapport with interviewers
- Demonstrates integrity and self-awareness
- Allows interviewers to assess genuine capabilities
- Reduces stress from trying to maintain fabrications
- Focuses discussion on actual strengths

**Disadvantages:**
- May feel vulnerable admitting knowledge gaps
- Could lead to missed opportunities if interviewers are inflexible
- Requires confidence to acknowledge limitations
- May not work with interviewers who expect complete answers

**Common Misconceptions:**
- Believing you must answer every question correctly to get hired
- Thinking interviewers expect expert-level knowledge in all areas
- Assuming self-ratings are evaluations rather than topic selection tools
- Fearing that admitting unknowns will disqualify you

## Q5
**Question:** What is the fifth tip for interview preparation, and why does it matter?

**Answer:** Study the company's core values and incorporate them into your answers. Mentioning aspects of the company's principles during responses shows you've researched the company and resonates with interviewers. Each company has specific leadership principles or values—study Amazon's leadership principles, Facebook's core values, or Tesla's leadership principles before interviewing there.

**Ideal Interview Answer:**
Research and internalize the target company's core values or leadership principles before the interview. When answering behavioral or situational questions, naturally weave in relevant company values. This demonstrates genuine interest in the organization and cultural alignment beyond just technical fit.

**Underlying Concept:**
Companies hire for cultural fit as much as technical ability. Demonstrating familiarity with and alignment to company values shows you've done homework and are likely to integrate successfully into their culture.

**Real-World Use Cases:**
- Using Amazon's "Customer Obsession" principle when describing how you prioritized user experience in a previous project
- Incorporating "Ownership" by discussing how you took responsibility beyond your assigned role
- Demonstrating "Bias for Action" when explaining how you moved quickly to resolve a critical production issue

**Advantages:**
- Shows genuine interest in the specific company
- Demonstrates cultural awareness and alignment
- Differentiates you from candidates who only focus on technical preparation
- Creates positive emotional connection with interviewers
- Indicates you've researched beyond surface-level company information

**Disadvantages:**
- Can seem insincere if not naturally integrated into answers
- Requires understanding not just the values but how they're applied
- May not resonate if the interviewer doesn't value cultural aspects
- Time investment to thoroughly understand and practice incorporating values

**Common Misconceptions:**
- Treating values as a checklist to mention rather than genuine alignment
- Memorizing values without understanding their practical application
- Using values inappropriately or forcing them into technical discussions
- Assuming all interviewers prioritize cultural fit equally
